Source of energy:

Complementary inputs from rooftop solar PV arrays and a small wind turbine (3‚Äì5 kW). Solar provides daytime energy, while wind often produces at night; hybridization increases availability and reduces storage needs.

Conversion process:

PV modules produce DC that is routed through MPPT controllers. Turbine output (AC) is rectified and conditioned. A hybrid energy management controller coordinates inputs, charges a battery bank through a BMS, and feeds an inverter that supplies AC to critical clinic loads. Designed to power vaccine refrigeration (cold chain), lighting, diagnostics, communications, and emergency systems at a rural health clinic. Batteries sized to guarantee autonomy for required hours during low generation periods.

Reduces diesel consumption and operating costs for healthcare facilities in remote areas; improves uptime for life-critical systems and supports community resilience.

Source of energy:

OTEC harnesses the temperature difference between warm surface seawater and cold deep seawater. Tropical ocean regions with stable thermal gradients (‚â• 20¬∞C at depth) are most suitable.

Conversion process:

Warm surface water evaporates a low-boiling working fluid (ORC), driving a turbine-generator. Cold deep seawater condenses the vapor in a heat exchanger and the working fluid is recirculated. Closed-loop (ORC) systems reduce environmental impact.

Output / utilization:

Base-load electricity generation for coastal communities, plus co-benefits like seawater air-conditioning, desalination, and aquaculture. OTEC plants can provide continuous power independent of weather.

Real-world relevance:

Promising for tropical islands and coastal regions with deep continental shelves; provides stable renewable power and desalinated water ‚Äî beneficial for energy-poor island nations.

Design notes: Requires deep water close to shore or long intake pipes, corrosion-resistant materials, and environmental assessment to minimize ecological impacts.
